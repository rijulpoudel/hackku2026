# LAUNCH — Scene Design Document
### Choose Your Financial Adventure: Life After Graduation
**Security Benefit Track — HackKU 2026**

---

## Overview

Four characters. Four completely different lives after graduation. Every scene teaches one real financial concept through a decision with real consequences. Players age 22 → 33 across 12 scenes.

**Design principle:** No scene should feel like a quiz. Every choice should feel real and hard.

# CHARACTER 1: MAYA CHEN — The Grad Student

**Background:** Maya graduated with a Biology degree and got into a fully-funded PhD program in biochemistry. She has a $28,000/year stipend, free tuition, and health insurance through the university. Her undergrad loans are deferred. She's smart, disciplined, and completely unprepared for real-world money.

**Core tension:** Living on a poverty-level stipend while deferring loans that are still accruing interest. The academic path has enormous upside but requires 5–7 years of sacrifice. Every dollar matters.

**What players learn:** Loan interest accrual during deferment, side income taxes, Roth IRA on low income, the real cost of a PhD, negotiating your first post-PhD salary.

---

### Scene 1 — Age 22: "Your Stipend Arrives"
**Setting:** Maya gets her first $2,333 stipend check (monthly). She expected it to feel like a salary. It doesn't.
**Decision:** How do you handle money on a $28k/year stipend?
- **Best:** Live with 2 roommates, track every dollar, open a Roth IRA ($50/month is enough at this income)
- **Smart:** Live alone in a cheap place, focus on research, skip investing for now
- **Risky:** Treat the stipend like a real salary — nice apartment, new laptop, eat out often
- **Danger:** Apply for credit cards to "supplement" the stipend
- **Concept taught:** Roth IRA contribution limits, why low-income years are the BEST time to fund a Roth (low tax bracket now = tax-free growth later)
- **Fact:** A $6,000 Roth IRA contribution at age 22 grows to ~$91,000 by age 65 at 7% — tax free.

---

### Scene 2 — Age 23: "Interest Never Sleeps"
**Setting:** Maya's been in the program a year. She assumed her loans were "paused." She checks her balance. It's higher than when she started.
**Decision:** Your deferred loans accrued $2,312 in interest this year. What do you do?
- **Best:** Pay just the interest every month ($192/month) so the balance doesn't grow
- **Smart:** Refinance to income-driven repayment and make small payments
- **Neutral:** Leave it deferred — deal with it after graduation
- **Danger:** Ignore it entirely and focus on dissertation
- **Concept taught:** Capitalized interest — when unpaid interest gets added to principal, you pay interest on interest
- **Fact:** $34,000 in loans at 6.5% accrues $2,210/year in interest during deferment. Over 5 years of a PhD, that's $11,050 added to your balance before you make a single payment.

---

### Scene 3 — Age 24: "The Side Hustle Question"
**Setting:** A biotech startup reaches out. They want Maya to do weekend consulting at $75/hour. She could earn $800–$1,500/month extra.
**Decision:** Do you take the consulting work?
- **Best:** Take it, but immediately set aside 25% of every payment for self-employment taxes (quarterly filing required)
- **Smart:** Take it and track income carefully — file quarterly estimated taxes
- **Risky:** Take the money, spend it freely, deal with taxes in April
- **Danger:** Ignore reporting it — "it's just side money, the IRS won't know"
- **Concept taught:** 1099 income, self-employment tax (15.3%), quarterly estimated payments, Schedule C
- **Fact:** Self-employed people owe BOTH the employer and employee share of Social Security and Medicare — 15.3% on top of income tax. Forgetting this is one of the most common first-year tax disasters.

---

### Scene 4 — Age 25: "Conference vs. Savings"
**Setting:** Maya's advisor wants her to attend a major conference in Boston. Cost: $1,800 (flight, hotel, registration). Her department will cover $600. She has $1,200 in savings.
**Decision:** How do you fund the remaining $1,200?
- **Best:** Negotiate with the department for more funding, apply for the conference travel grant (they exist — you just have to ask)
- **Smart:** Pay the $1,200 from savings, rebuild the fund over 4 months
- **Risky:** Put it on a credit card and pay it off over 6 months (interest: ~$80)
- **Danger:** Skip the conference — networking isn't worth going broke
- **Concept taught:** Emergency fund vs. opportunity fund, the ROI of professional networking, travel grant applications
- **Fact:** Academic conferences are where 60% of post-PhD job offers originate. The $1,800 investment has an expected return of $8,000–$15,000 in first-year salary difference.

---

### Scene 5 — Age 26: "The Offer to Leave"
**Setting:** A biotech company saw Maya's published research. They offer her a full-time research scientist job: $95,000, full benefits, 401k with 4% match. She's only halfway through her PhD.
**Decision:** Do you leave the PhD for the job?
- **Best:** Negotiate a leave of absence, take the job, reassess after 1 year (keeps options open)
- **Smart:** Decline and finish the PhD — the credential is worth $20–40k in lifetime salary premium in academia
- **Neutral:** Ask for a deferred start date — finish the dissertation in 18 months, then join
- **Risky:** Drop out immediately without negotiating anything
- **Concept taught:** Opportunity cost of a credential, salary premium of advanced degrees, the real value of finishing what you started
- **Fact:** PhD holders in STEM earn 26% more than master's degree holders over a lifetime. But the 5-year income gap during the PhD = ~$280,000 in forgone earnings. The math depends on your field.

---

### Scene 6 — Age 27: "Defending and Graduating"
**Setting:** Maya defends her dissertation. She's officially Dr. Chen. Her deferred loans are about to enter repayment — $41,200 (original $34k + accrued interest). She has 3 job offers.
**Decision:** Which job do you take?
- **Best:** University research position ($72k) — qualifies for Public Service Loan Forgiveness after 10 years. Over time: best loan outcome.
- **Smart:** Biotech company ($105k) — high salary, can aggressively pay down loans in 4 years
- **Neutral:** Government research lab ($85k) — middle ground, also PSLF eligible
- **Risky:** Academic postdoc ($48k) — prestigious, but another 2 years of poverty wages
- **Concept taught:** Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF), income-driven repayment strategy, comparing total compensation not just salary
- **Fact:** Under PSLF, after 10 years of qualifying payments in a public sector job, the remaining balance is forgiven TAX-FREE. For $41,200 in loans, this could mean $30,000+ forgiven.

---

### Scene 7 — Age 28: "First Real Salary, First Real Taxes"
**Setting:** First job after the PhD. First W-2 with a real salary. First time filing taxes that actually owe something.
**Decision:** Your tax bill is $4,200. Your refund last year was $800. What happened?
- **Best:** Update W-4 to withhold correctly, set up a $200/month tax savings account going forward
- **Smart:** Pay the $4,200, file correctly, adjust withholding for next year
- **Risky:** Set up a payment plan with the IRS (adds fees and interest)
- **Danger:** Ignore the bill — the IRS will find you, add 5%/month penalties, and potentially garnish wages
- **Concept taught:** How income tax brackets work with a sudden salary jump, W-4 adjustments, underpayment penalties
- **Fact:** Moving from $28k to $72k in one year can spike your effective tax rate from 10% to 22%. Most people don't adjust withholding in time and get hit with a surprise bill.

---

### Scene 8 — Age 29: "The Lab or the Startup?"
**Setting:** Maya's research gets attention. A venture capital firm wants to fund a startup around her discovery. She'd be co-founder + CTO. Salary: $60k (below current). Equity: 8%.
**Decision:** Do you leave stable employment to co-found the startup?
- **Best:** Negotiate: keep current job part-time as advisor, take smaller equity stake with vesting schedule
- **Smart:** Take the leap — negotiate salary to $75k, take the equity
- **Risky:** Accept the $60k cut without negotiating equity terms
- **Danger:** Sign whatever they offer without reading the vesting cliff terms
- **Concept taught:** Startup equity, vesting schedules, the 4-year cliff, dilution, how equity actually works (vs. how movies portray it)
- **Fact:** 90% of startup equity never pays out. But the 10% that does changes lives. Always negotiate the vesting schedule — a 1-year cliff means you get nothing if the company fails in month 11.

---

### Scene 9 — Age 30: "PSLF Checkpoint"
**Setting:** Maya chose the university job. She's 4 years into PSLF — 6 years remain. Her income-driven payments are $312/month. A private lender offers to refinance her loans to 4.5% — she'd save $180/month immediately.
**Decision:** Do you refinance your federal loans to private?
- **Best:** Stay in PSLF. In 6 years, $29,000 gets forgiven. The refinance "savings" ($12,960 over 6 years) is less than the forgiveness.
- **Smart:** Run the math — at her balance, PSLF wins by $16,000. Stay the course.
- **Danger:** Refinance to private. You just permanently exited PSLF. Those 4 years of qualifying payments are gone.
- **Concept taught:** PSLF vs. refinancing trade-off, the math of loan forgiveness vs. interest savings, why you can NEVER go back to federal programs after refinancing
- **Fact:** In 2023, the Education Department forgave $42 billion in loans through PSLF for 615,000 borrowers. The average forgiveness was $68,000 per borrower.

---

### Scene 10 — Age 31: "Buying vs. Renting on an Academic Salary"
**Setting:** Maya wants to buy a home. Academic town means homes are $280,000. She's been renting for $950/month. Mortgage would be $1,450/month (20% down = $56,000 she'd need to save).
**Decision:** Do you buy a home now or keep renting?
- **Best:** Keep renting — she only has $22k saved, and academic positions aren't guaranteed. Build to full 20% down first.
- **Smart:** Buy with 10% down, pay PMI ($180/month) until 20% equity is reached
- **Risky:** Buy with 3.5% FHA loan — lower down payment but higher monthly cost and PMI for years
- **Danger:** Drain retirement accounts for the down payment to avoid PMI
- **Concept taught:** PMI, down payment thresholds, 20% rule, total cost of ownership vs. renting
- **Fact:** PMI typically costs 0.5%–1.5% of the loan per year. On a $252,000 loan, that's $1,260–$3,780/year until you reach 20% equity — money that builds zero wealth.

---

### Scene 11 — Age 32: "PSLF Finish Line"
**Setting:** Maya reaches 120 qualifying payments. Her remaining balance: $24,800. The forgiveness is approved — tax-free.
**Decision:** You just freed up $312/month. What do you do with it?
- **Best:** Max out Roth IRA ($583/month), redirect $312 to index funds — she's 10 years behind on investing
- **Smart:** Split: $200 to retirement, $112 to emergency fund (she only has 2 months saved)
- **Neutral:** Put it all in a savings account — "I'll figure out investing later"
- **Risky:** Upgrade lifestyle — she's earned it, time for a nicer apartment
- **Concept taught:** Redirecting debt payments to wealth building, the power of automating investing, catching up on retirement savings in your 30s
- **Fact:** Investing $312/month starting at 32 at 7% annual return = $283,000 by age 65. Starting at 22 with the same amount = $884,000. That's the cost of a decade of loans.

---

### Scene 12 — Age 33: "The Tenure Question"
**Setting:** Maya is up for tenure review. If she gets it — lifetime job security, pension, $95k salary. If she doesn't — she has 1 year to find a new position. Her net worth: $48,000 (retirement) + $12,000 cash.
**Decision:** You don't get tenure. What's your plan?
- **Best:** She had an emergency fund — 4 months of expenses. Activate it, apply aggressively, negotiate the best possible exit package from the university.
- **Smart:** Pivot to industry — her skills are worth $120k+ in biotech. Make the leap.
- **Risky:** Take the first offer that comes — a research position at $72k in a low-cost-of-living city
- **Danger:** Panic, pull from retirement accounts to cover living expenses during the job search
- **Concept taught:** Career risk management, the role of an emergency fund as career insurance, early retirement withdrawal penalties (10% + income tax)
- **Fact:** Only 20% of PhD graduates in STEM get tenure-track positions. The average academic job search takes 14 months. An emergency fund isn't optional — it's career infrastructure.

# CHARACTER 2: ALEX RIVERA — The Corporate Climber

**Background:** Alex graduated with a Computer Science degree and landed a job at a mid-size tech company in Austin, TX. $58,000 salary, full benefits, 401k with 5% match. On paper: the dream. In practice: Alex has never had real money before and has no idea what to do with it.

**Core tension:** High enough income to make real wealth-building decisions early — but also high enough to make expensive mistakes that compound for decades. The classic "lifestyle inflation trap."

**What players learn:** 401k matching, salary negotiation, renting vs. buying, lifestyle inflation, investing basics, navigating a layoff.

---

### Scene 1 — Age 22: "First Paycheck Shock"
**Setting:** Alex's first paycheck is $1,823. Expected ~$2,400. The missing $577 is taxes, benefits, and 401k contributions.
**Decision:** The $380/month 401k deduction feels painful. Do you reduce it?
- **Best:** Keep the full 5% contribution — employer matches every dollar. That's a 100% return before the market does anything.
- **Smart:** Reduce to 3% — get partial match, keep more cash for now
- **Neutral:** Pause it entirely — "I'll start next year when I'm more settled"
- **Concept taught:** 401k employer match, pre-tax contributions, the actual cost of opting out
- **Fact:** An employee who skips a 5% match on a $58k salary loses $2,900/year in free money. Over 10 years at 7% growth, that's $40,000 in forgone wealth.

---

### Scene 2 — Age 23: "The Car Upgrade"
**Setting:** Alex's old car dies. Needs a replacement. Has $4,000 saved. A dealership approves a loan for a $34,000 SUV at 7.5% interest for 72 months.
**Decision:** What car do you buy?
- **Best:** Buy a reliable 3-year-old used car for $14,000 — $10k down, small loan, paid off in 2 years
- **Smart:** Lease a modest new car — lower monthly payments, always covered by warranty
- **Risky:** Buy the $34k SUV — monthly payment is $594, which is 13% of take-home pay
- **Danger:** Max out the loan to get the $40k model — "I deserve it, I work hard"
- **Concept taught:** Total cost of a car loan (principal + interest), the 20% rule for car purchases (down payment), total car cost as % of income
- **Fact:** A $34,000 car loan at 7.5% for 72 months costs $42,800 total. The same $34,000 invested at 7% for 6 years grows to $51,000. The car vs. investing gap: $93,800.

---

### Scene 3 — Age 24: "Apartment vs. Starter Home"
**Setting:** Alex's lease is up. Austin is expensive. Option A: Renew the lease at $1,450/month. Option B: Buy a $320,000 starter home with 5% down ($16,000).
**Decision:** Do you buy your first home?
- **Best:** Rent another year — Alex has $14k saved, which isn't enough for a proper down payment plus closing costs ($8k–$12k)
- **Smart:** Buy — lock in a payment before rates rise further, build equity
- **Risky:** Buy with only 3.5% FHA down — lower down payment but higher overall cost
- **Danger:** Buy with 0 down using a VA-style program without fully understanding the terms
- **Concept taught:** Closing costs, PMI, the full cost of buying (not just the down payment), the 5-year break-even rule for buying vs. renting
- **Fact:** Buying a home costs an average of $12,000 in closing costs alone — before the down payment. Most first-time buyers don't budget for this.

---

### Scene 4 — Age 25: "The Salary Negotiation"
**Setting:** Alex has been at the company 3 years. Annual review time. Manager offers a 4% raise ($2,320). A recruiter reached out last week with a role at $82,000.
**Decision:** How do you respond to the 4% raise offer?
- **Best:** Counter with data — market rate for your role is $78k. Ask for $76k or you'll need to explore external options.
- **Smart:** Accept and immediately schedule a conversation about a promotion path
- **Neutral:** Accept the 4% without negotiating — "It's something"
- **Risky:** Quit on the spot without another offer in hand
- **Concept taught:** Salary negotiation, market rate research (Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, LinkedIn Salary), the lifetime cost of not negotiating
- **Fact:** Employees who negotiate their first salary earn $5,000–$10,000 more per year. Compounded over a 40-year career, that difference is worth $1–2 million in lifetime earnings.

---

### Scene 5 — Age 26: "The 'Fun' Credit Card"
**Setting:** Alex has a rewards credit card with a $12,000 limit. A Europe trip with friends costs $4,800. Alex doesn't have the cash — but the card is there.
**Decision:** Do you put the Europe trip on the credit card?
- **Best:** Don't go — save for 6 months and take the trip next year, cash only
- **Smart:** Go, but only charge $2,400 (half) and pay it off in 3 months
- **Risky:** Charge the full $4,800, pay minimums — "I'll figure it out"
- **Danger:** Charge the trip AND book an upgrade — YOLO. The APR is 24%.
- **Concept taught:** Credit card APR, minimum payment trap, the true cost of revolving credit card debt
- **Fact:** $4,800 at 24% APR paying only minimums takes 22 years to pay off and costs $7,400 in interest. The Europe trip ends up costing $12,200.

---

### Scene 6 — Age 27: "Layoff Season"
**Setting:** The company announces 15% workforce reduction. Alex survives — but three teammates didn't. Alex's emergency fund: $3,200 (about 5 weeks of expenses).
**Decision:** You weren't laid off, but this scared you. What do you do next?
- **Best:** Aggressively build the emergency fund to 6 months ($17,400) before any other financial goals
- **Smart:** Split: 70% of extra savings to emergency fund, 30% to investments
- **Neutral:** Keep investing normally — "I still have my job, I'm fine"
- **Risky:** Do nothing and spend the $3,200 on a weekend trip to "decompress"
- **Concept taught:** Emergency fund sizing (3–6 months of expenses), job security as a financial variable, the psychological role of a cash cushion
- **Fact:** The average job search after a tech layoff takes 3–5 months. With no emergency fund, most people resort to credit cards within 6 weeks. The average credit card debt accrued during unemployment: $4,500.

---

### Scene 7 — Age 28: "Stock Options Vest"
**Setting:** Alex's company stock options just vested: 500 shares at a strike price of $12. Current price: $38/share. Paper value: $13,000.
**Decision:** What do you do with the vested options?
- **Best:** Exercise and sell immediately — pay the tax, diversify into index funds. Concentration risk is real.
- **Smart:** Exercise half, sell, diversify. Keep half for potential upside.
- **Risky:** Keep all 500 shares — "The stock is going up, I know this company"
- **Danger:** Don't exercise at all — let them expire worthless because the paperwork is confusing
- **Concept taught:** Stock options vs. RSUs, strike price, concentration risk, capital gains tax (short-term vs long-term)
- **Fact:** Enron employees had 60% of their 401k in company stock. When it collapsed, they lost everything — retirement savings and job simultaneously. Diversification isn't a preference; it's risk management.

---

### Scene 8 — Age 29: "The Wedding Budget"
**Setting:** Alex is getting married. Partner wants a $45,000 wedding. Alex wants to keep it under $20,000. Combined savings: $28,000.
**Decision:** How do you handle the wedding cost?
- **Best:** Set a firm $22,000 budget. Prioritize what matters most. Don't touch retirement savings.
- **Smart:** Have an honest conversation — scale the wedding, invest the difference as a couple
- **Risky:** Spend $38,000 — drain most savings, "we'll rebuild"
- **Danger:** Finance $25,000 of it with a personal loan at 11% APR
- **Concept taught:** Couples and money, the financial conversation before marriage, the opportunity cost of a wedding (what $20,000 invested grows to)
- **Fact:** The average US wedding costs $30,000. $30,000 invested at age 29 at 7% grows to $160,000 by retirement. Every dollar spent on the wedding is a dollar not compounding for 35 years.

---

### Scene 9 — Age 30: "Buying the House (For Real This Time)"
**Setting:** Alex and partner combined income: $128,000. They have $58,000 saved. Austin housing market: $420,000 for a 3BR. Mortgage: ~$2,100/month.
**Decision:** Do you buy now or wait?
- **Best:** Buy with 20% down ($84,000) — wait 18 more months to save the rest, avoid PMI
- **Smart:** Buy now with $58,000 down (13.8%) — pay PMI until 20% equity, then refinance
- **Risky:** Buy the $480k house — it's "nicer" and "an investment"
- **Danger:** Drain both retirement accounts for a larger down payment to avoid PMI
- **Concept taught:** Combined income home buying, 28% housing rule (mortgage shouldn't exceed 28% of gross monthly income), PMI removal process
- **Fact:** At a combined $128k income, the safe mortgage is $2,987/month max (28% rule). $2,100 is well under that. But the down payment timing is what separates the smart buy from the risky one.

---

### Scene 10 — Age 31: "The Promotion or the Jump"
**Setting:** Alex is offered a Senior Engineer promotion: +$14k salary ($92k total). Same week, a startup recruiter offers $130k base + equity.
**Decision:** Stable promotion or startup leap?
- **Best:** Take the startup — negotiate $130k base, 0.8% equity, 4-year vest, 1-year cliff. Run the math on equity dilution first.
- **Smart:** Take the promotion — stability, 401k match, known culture. The $38k gap is real but so is startup failure rate.
- **Risky:** Take the startup without reading the equity terms or understanding the vesting schedule
- **Neutral:** Counter the current employer with the startup offer — see if they'll match
- **Concept taught:** Total compensation analysis, startup equity math, opportunity cost of stability
- **Fact:** 90% of startups fail within 10 years. But startup employees at the remaining 10% see equity payouts averaging $380,000. The math only works if you understand the equity terms before you sign.

---

### Scene 11 — Age 32: "Maxing Out Tax-Advantaged Accounts"
**Setting:** Alex and partner are both working, combined income $195k. They're in the 22% bracket. An accountant tells them they're leaving money on the table.
**Decision:** Which accounts do you prioritize?
- **Best:** Max 401k ($23,500 each = $47,000), max HSA ($8,300 family), then taxable brokerage
- **Smart:** Max both 401ks, skip HSA (healthy, low medical costs)
- **Neutral:** Contribute to 401k to the match only, invest the rest in taxable brokerage
- **Risky:** Skip retirement accounts — "we make good money, we'll invest later"
- **Concept taught:** Tax-advantaged account hierarchy (401k → HSA → Roth IRA → taxable), HSA triple tax advantage, why high earners need pre-tax accounts most
- **Fact:** A family maxing their 401ks at $195k income saves $10,340 in federal taxes per year compared to investing in a taxable account. Over 20 years, that tax savings compounds to $650,000.

---

### Scene 12 — Age 33: "The Financial Picture at 33"
**Setting:** Alex is 33. Net worth: $210,000 (retirement + equity + home). Student loans: paid off 2 years ago. One kid, thinking about college savings.
**Decision:** Do you start a 529 college savings plan?
- **Best:** Open a 529 now — even $200/month at 7% over 18 years = $87,000 tax-free for college
- **Smart:** Open it, contribute $100/month — better than nothing
- **Neutral:** Skip it — "college is 18 years away"
- **Risky:** Put the college fund in a taxable brokerage — "more flexibility"
- **Concept taught:** 529 plans, state tax deductions for 529 contributions, compound growth over 18 years, the difference between 529 and taxable accounts for education
- **Fact:** College costs have increased 8% per year on average since 1980. A public university that costs $28,000/year today will cost $93,000/year in 2042. A 529 funded from birth is the only realistic way to avoid a repeat of the student loan crisis.

# CHARACTER 3: JORDAN KIM — The Freelance Creative

**Background:** Jordan graduated with a Graphic Design degree and immediately went freelance — no job applications, no corporate life. Building a client base from scratch with $0 in savings, $36,000 in loans, and a gig economy that doesn't come with a rulebook.

**Core tension:** Variable income makes every financial decision harder. Taxes, health insurance, retirement — all of it falls entirely on Jordan. No employer to catch anything. High freedom, high responsibility.

**What players learn:** Quarterly taxes, SEP-IRA vs Roth IRA, health insurance marketplace, building income stability, invoice management, the feast-or-famine cycle.

---

### Scene 1 — Age 22: "First Client, First Invoice"
**Setting:** Jordan lands a brand identity project: $3,500. The client pays after 45 days. Jordan spent the money before it arrived.
**Decision:** How do you manage cash flow as a freelancer?
- **Best:** Create a "runway account" — always keep 2 months of expenses saved so late invoices don't break you
- **Smart:** Net-15 payment terms on all invoices, small late fee for overdue accounts
- **Risky:** Accept net-60 terms for bigger clients — the money is good but it's slow
- **Danger:** Trust verbal agreements, no written contracts, no invoice system
- **Concept taught:** Freelance cash flow management, invoice terms (net-15, net-30, net-60), contracts as financial protection
- **Fact:** 29% of small business failures are caused by cash flow problems — not lack of clients. Late payments are the #1 cause of freelancer financial crises.

---

### Scene 2 — Age 23: "The April Surprise"
**Setting:** Jordan earned $42,000 last year from freelance work. Never paid quarterly taxes. Gets a letter from the IRS: owes $8,400 plus a $380 underpayment penalty.
**Decision:** How do you handle this and prevent it next year?
- **Best:** Pay the $8,780, set up a dedicated tax savings account — deposit 28% of every client payment immediately
- **Smart:** Pay with a payment plan, set calendar reminders for quarterly filing dates (Apr 15, Jun 15, Sep 15, Jan 15)
- **Risky:** Put the tax bill on a credit card, pay it off over 6 months
- **Danger:** Ignore the letter — "they'll work something out"
- **Concept taught:** Self-employment tax (15.3%), quarterly estimated payments, how freelance income gets taxed differently
- **Fact:** Freelancers pay 15.3% self-employment tax ON TOP OF income tax. On $42,000 of freelance income, total tax owed is approximately $9,800 — money many freelancers don't set aside.

---

### Scene 3 — Age 24: "Health Insurance on Your Own"
**Setting:** Jordan's parents' insurance ends at 26 — wait, they said the plan ends now because Jordan is no longer a dependent. Open enrollment is in 2 weeks.
**Decision:** How do you get health insurance as a freelancer?
- **Best:** Shop the Healthcare.gov marketplace — at $42k income, Jordan qualifies for subsidized plans. Best value: silver plan ~$180/month after subsidy.
- **Smart:** Join a freelancer union or professional association that offers group rates
- **Risky:** Buy the cheapest bronze plan — low premium, but $7,000 deductible is a financial trap
- **Danger:** Go uninsured — "I'm young and healthy, nothing will happen"
- **Concept taught:** ACA marketplace, premium tax credits, metal tiers (bronze/silver/gold), deductibles vs premiums, the real cost of being uninsured
- **Fact:** The average uninsured emergency room visit costs $2,200. A 3-day hospital stay averages $30,000. One accident without insurance can take a decade to financially recover from.

---

### Scene 4 — Age 25: "The Feast Month"
**Setting:** Jordan's best month ever: $11,000 in client payments. Normally earns $3,500–$5,000/month. This feels like a raise. Expensive dinner. New equipment. Weekend trip.
**Decision:** You made $11,000 this month. How do you allocate it?
- **Best:** Treat it like a normal $4,500 month. Put $3,000 in a "famine fund" for slow months. Pay quarterly taxes on the full amount.
- **Smart:** Invest $2,000 in a SEP-IRA (tax deductible), put $1,000 extra toward loans
- **Risky:** Upgrade lifestyle — "I finally deserve it"
- **Danger:** Spend most of it, forget to set aside taxes, get hit with penalties next quarter
- **Concept taught:** Income smoothing for variable earners, SEP-IRA (freelancers can contribute up to 25% of net income), the feast-or-famine cycle
- **Fact:** Freelancers earn 30% more per hour on average than employees — but 40% end up with less net wealth due to irregular saving, missed tax payments, and no employer-funded benefits.

---

### Scene 5 — Age 26: "A Retainer Client"
**Setting:** A startup offers Jordan a 6-month retainer: $4,500/month guaranteed, 20 hours/week. It's less than Jordan's hourly rate ($95/hour for project work), but it's stable.
**Decision:** Do you take the retainer?
- **Best:** Accept, but negotiate the rate to $5,200/month and keep 2 project slots open for better-paying work
- **Smart:** Take it as-is — stability has real value when your income is unpredictable
- **Risky:** Decline — "I can make more per hour on projects"

- **Concept taught:** Value of income stability, diversified client base, retainer pricing strategy, not putting all eggs in one client basket

- **Concept taught:** SEP-IRA vs Solo 401k contribution limits, self-employed retirement account options, the tax deduction for retirement contributions
- **Fact:** A freelancer contributing $12,000/year to a SEP-IRA at 27 at 7% growth will have $1.1M by age 65. Contributing nothing until 37 and doing the same yields $541,000. Ten years = $560,000 difference.

- **Fact:** Withdrawing $10,000 from a retirement account early costs approximately $3,700 in penalties and taxes. That same $10,000 left invested for 20 years at 7% = $38,700. Early withdrawal is one of the most expensive financial decisions you can make.

- **Fact:** An S-Corp election on $85,000 freelance income can save $4,200/year in self-employment taxes. The $1,200/year in LLC fees pay for themselves 3.5x. Most freelancers don't discover this until a CPA tells them at year 5+.

# CHARACTER 4: SAM PATEL — The Public Servant

**Background:** Sam graduated with an Education degree and became a public school teacher in their hometown. $38,000 starting salary, full benefits, pension, and most importantly — Public Service Loan Forgiveness eligibility from day one. Low income on paper. But if played right, Sam's path is quietly the most financially secure of the four.

**Core tension:** Living on a teacher's salary requires discipline. But Sam has access to PSLF, a pension, and low cost-of-living advantages that most high-earners don't. The lesson: income isn't the only variable in the wealth equation.

**What players learn:** PSLF, teacher loan forgiveness, pension vs. 401k, income-driven repayment, side income as a teacher, the power of low expenses.

---

### Scene 1 — Age 22: "Teacher Pay Reality Check"
**Setting:** Sam's first paycheck: $1,246 bi-weekly ($29,900/year after taxes). The lunch crowd at school talks about how teachers are underpaid. Sam is starting to agree.
**Decision:** How do you build a financial life on a teacher's salary?
- **Best:** Enroll in income-driven repayment (SAVE plan) immediately — on $38k, monthly payment is ~$87/month. Start PSLF clock on day one.
- **Smart:** Pick the cheapest repayment plan to keep cash flowing, investigate PSLF eligibility
- **Neutral:** Default to standard 10-year repayment — $376/month, but pays off faster
- **Danger:** Ignore loans — "teacher salaries are too low to pay these anyway"
- **Concept taught:** Income-Driven Repayment plans (SAVE, PAYE, IBR), PSLF qualification requirements, why enrolling on day one matters
- **Fact:** Teachers who enroll in PSLF on their first day of work and stay in public education for 10 years can have 100% of their remaining federal loans forgiven — tax free. The average teacher forgiveness: $47,000.

---

### Scene 2 — Age 23: "The Side Hustle Every Teacher Has"
**Setting:** Sam starts tutoring on weekends — $60/hour, 3–4 students per week. That's $720–$960/month extra. It's life-changing on a teacher salary.
**Decision:** What do you do with the tutoring income?
- **Best:** Set aside 28% for taxes (it's self-employment income), invest the rest in a Roth IRA — Sam's income is low enough to make Roth contributions ideal
- **Smart:** Use it to build an emergency fund to 3 months of expenses ($8,400)
- **Risky:** Spend it freely — "I earned this, I deserve to enjoy it"
- **Danger:** Don't report it — "it's just cash, no one tracks tutoring"
- **Concept taught:** Side income taxation, Roth IRA vs Traditional IRA at low income, 1099 income from tutoring
- **Fact:** Teachers who invest their tutoring income ($800/month at 7% for 10 years) accumulate $138,000 — which the PSLF forgiveness adds on top of. Combined, this is why the teacher path is more financially powerful than it looks.

---

### Scene 3 — Age 24: "The Pension Conversation"
**Setting:** HR gives Sam a packet: the school district pension. Sam contributes 6% of salary, district contributes 14%. After 30 years, Sam gets 65% of final salary forever.
**Decision:** A financial advisor says to skip the pension and invest in a 403b instead. What do you do?
- **Best:** Keep the pension AND open a 403b — contribute $200/month to the 403b for additional retirement security
- **Smart:** Stay with the pension — it's a guaranteed income stream in retirement. The 403b is bonus.
- **Risky:** Opt out of the pension to control your own investments — "I can do better than 65% of salary"
- **Danger:** Never think about retirement because "the pension covers it"
- **Concept taught:** Defined benefit pension vs. defined contribution 401k/403b, pension vesting schedules, why pensions are valuable and rare
- **Fact:** Only 15% of private sector workers have a pension. Teachers who stay 30 years can retire with $24,700/year guaranteed income (65% of $38k) adjusted for raises. That's equivalent to having $617,000 saved.

---

### Scene 4 — Age 25: "Cost of Living Decision"
**Setting:** A neighboring district in a bigger city offers Sam a job: $52,000/year (+$14k). But rent there is $1,600/month vs $750/month in the hometown. And the new job is not PSLF-qualifying.
**Decision:** Do you take the higher-paying job?
- **Best:** Decline — the $14k raise is wiped out by the $850/month rent increase ($10,200/year) AND loses PSLF eligibility
- **Smart:** Negotiate a higher salary at the current district first, using the offer as leverage
- **Risky:** Take the job — "more money is more money"
- **Neutral:** Ask the new district if they're a PSLF-qualifying employer first
- **Concept taught:** Total compensation math (salary vs cost of living), PSLF disqualification risk, the hidden value of staying in public service
- **Fact:** A teacher earning $38k in a low cost-of-living area can have a higher effective standard of living than one earning $55k in a high cost-of-living city — after accounting for rent, taxes, and commuting costs.

---

### Scene 5 — Age 26: "Building Wealth on a Teacher's Salary"
**Setting:** Sam's expenses are low. After rent ($750), car ($0 — paid off), food, and loan payment ($87), there's $400–$600 left each month. What do you do with it?
**Decision:** How do you invest $500/month on a teacher's salary?
- **Best:** Max Roth IRA ($583/month) — at Sam's income, every dollar grows tax-free, and contributions (not earnings) can be withdrawn penalty-free
- **Smart:** Split: $300 to Roth IRA, $200 to emergency fund until it hits 6 months
- **Neutral:** Put it all in a savings account — "I'll figure out investing later"
- **Risky:** Invest in individual stocks — "I've been reading about this one company"
- **Concept taught:** Roth IRA contribution limits, index fund investing, the power of consistent investing on a modest income
- **Fact:** $500/month invested from age 26 to 65 at 7% = $1.3 million. You don't need a high salary to build significant wealth — you need consistent habits and time.

### Scene 6 — Age 27: "PSLF Year 5 Checkpoint"
**Setting:** Sam is 5 years in — halfway to PSLF. Remaining loan balance: $29,400. A private lender sends a mailer: refinance to 4.9%, save $140/month on payments.
**Decision:** Do you refinance?
- **Best:** Absolutely not — refinancing federal loans to private permanently disqualifies you from PSLF. 5 years of progress would be erased.
- **Smart:** Call your loan servicer to confirm PSLF-qualifying payment count before making any decisions
- **Risky:** Refinance — the $140/month saving sounds compelling
- **Danger:** Refinance without understanding the PSLF consequences
- **Concept taught:** PSLF disqualification, the true cost of refinancing away from federal programs, confirming PSLF payment counts annually
- **Fact:** In 2022, thousands of borrowers discovered their loan servicer had given them incorrect information about PSLF eligibility. Always confirm your qualifying payment count annually through the PSLF Help Tool at studentaid.gov.

### Scene 7 — Age 28: "Summer as an Asset"

- **Best:** Teach 4 weeks of summer school ($4,200), spend the rest on rest and course development — balance recovery with income
- **Smart:** Build one online course (Teachers Pay Teachers, Udemy) — passive income that compounds while Sam works the school year
- **Neutral:** Relax fully — teacher burnout is real, rest is necessary
- **Risky:** Work all 10 weeks, skip vacation — risk burning out
- **Concept taught:** Passive income, teachers' unique summer income opportunity, burnout as a financial risk (career-ending burnout is common)
- **Fact:** The top Teachers Pay Teachers sellers earn $50,000–$200,000/year in passive income from curriculum materials. The average successful seller earns $5,000–$15,000/year — entirely in addition to their teaching salary.

### Scene 8 — Age 29: "The Engagement and Money Conversations"

- **Risky:** Assume everything will "work out" without having the money conversation
- **Danger:** One person secretly controls all finances

- **Fact:** Money is the #1 cause of divorce in the US — cited in 22% of all divorces. Couples who discuss finances weekly are 60% more likely to report being "happy" with their financial situation than those who never talk about money.

### Scene 9 — Age 30: "Buying a House on a Teacher's Salary"

- **Concept taught:** HUD Teacher Next Door program, down payment grants for teachers/nurses/first responders, the cost of PMI, keeping cash reserves after purchase

### Scene 10 — Age 31: "The Department Chair Offer"

- **Best:** Accept if it fits PSLF requirements (it does), negotiate a plan to return to full classroom in 3 years if leadership isn't fulfilling
